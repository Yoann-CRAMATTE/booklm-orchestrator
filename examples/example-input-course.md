## Chapter 1: What is Project Management? (Pages 1-12)

### 1.1 Definition & Core Concepts

Project management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet the project requirements. A project is a temporary endeavor undertaken to produce a unique product, service, or result.

Key characteristics:
- **Temporary**: Has a defined beginning and end
- **Unique**: Creates unique deliverables
- **Progressive Elaboration**: Developed in stages with increasing detail
- **Defined Objectives**: Clear goals and success criteria

### 1.2 The Triple Constraint (Iron Triangle)

Every project must balance three competing factors:

1. **Scope**: What will be delivered?
2. **Time**: When will it be delivered?
3. **Cost**: How much will it cost?

Managing projects involves making tradeoffs between these three factors.

### 1.3 Why Project Management Matters

- Reduces risk of failure
- Improves resource allocation
- Enables better communication
- Increases stakeholder satisfaction
- Helps achieve strategic objectives
- Provides predictability and control

### 1.4 Brief History of Project Management

Project management evolved from military and construction backgrounds:
- 1950s: Initial formalization in aerospace/defense
- 1960s: Critical Path Method (CPM) and PERT developed
- 1980s: Personal computers enable better tracking
- 1990s: Software becomes mainstream tool
- 2000s: Agile methodologies emerge
- Present: Hybrid approaches (Water-Scrum-Fall)

## Chapter 2: Frameworks & Methodologies (Pages 13-28)

### 2.1 Traditional (Waterfall) Approach

**Characteristics**:
- Sequential phases (Requirements → Design → Development → Testing → Deployment)
- Planning upfront
- Changes are expensive and difficult
- Works well for: Well-defined requirements, stable scope

**Advantages**:
- Clear deliverables
- Easy to track progress against plan
- Good for fixed-price contracts
- Works with large teams

**Disadvantages**:
- Late discovery of problems (testing phase)
- Customer sees product only at end
- Difficult to adapt to changes
- Long time to value

### 2.2 Agile Approach

**Characteristics**:
- Iterative development (2-4 week sprints)
- Continuous customer feedback
- Embrace change as competitive advantage
- Works well for: Uncertain requirements, innovation projects

**Key Agile Values** (from Agile Manifesto):
1. Individuals and interactions over processes
2. Working software over documentation
3. Customer collaboration over contracts
4. Responding to change over plans

**Popular Agile frameworks**:
- **Scrum**: Roles (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Team), ceremonies (standup, sprint)
- **Kanban**: Continuous flow, visualize work
- **Lean**: Eliminate waste, deliver value

**Advantages**:
- Early and frequent delivery of value
- Adaptable to change
- High team engagement
- Reduced risk of total failure

**Disadvantages**:
- Requires significant customer involvement
- Can lack documentation
- Difficult with distributed teams
- Scope creep risk

### 2.3 Choosing Your Approach

| Factor | Waterfall | Agile |
|--------|-----------|-------|
| Requirements clarity | High (stable) | Low (evolving) |
| Team size | Large | Small to medium |
| Customer availability | Low (upfront) | High (continuous) |
| Change frequency | Low | High |
| Risk tolerance | Low | Medium-high |
| Time-to-value | Long | Short |
| Documentation needs | High | Minimal |

### 2.4 Hybrid Approaches

Many organizations now use hybrid models:
- Water-Scrum-Fall: Waterfall for planning, Scrum for development, Waterfall for closure
- Disciplined Agile: Agile development with traditional governance
- SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework): Agile at scale for large enterprises

## Chapter 3: Planning & Scheduling (Pages 29-45)

### 3.1 Project Planning Basics

Project planning involves:
1. Define objectives and success criteria
2. Identify all work required (scope)
3. Estimate effort and duration
4. Sequence activities
5. Allocate resources
6. Identify risks

**Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)**:
- Hierarchical decomposition of all work
- Goes from high-level deliverables to detailed activities
- Ensures nothing is forgotten
- Forms basis for scheduling, budgeting, resource allocation

### 3.2 Scheduling Techniques

**Critical Path Method (CPM)**:
- Identifies longest sequence of dependent activities
- Critical path determines minimum project duration
- Any delay on critical path delays project
- Non-critical activities have "float" (schedule flexibility)

**Gantt Chart**:
- Visual timeline of activities
- Shows start/end dates
- Shows dependencies between activities
- Easy to communicate to stakeholders

### 3.3 Resource Planning

- Identify what resources needed (people, equipment, materials)
- Determine availability
- Level resources (avoid over/under allocation)
- Plan for contingency

## Chapter 4: Risk Management (Pages 46-62)

### 4.1 What is Risk?

Risk = Probability × Impact

A potential negative event that could affect project success. Risk management is about identifying, analyzing, and mitigating these potential problems before they occur.

### 4.2 Risk Management Process

1. **Identify Risks**: Brainstorm, historical data, expert opinion
2. **Analyze Risks**: Assess probability and impact
3. **Respond to Risks**: Avoid, mitigate, transfer, or accept
4. **Monitor Risks**: Track and control throughout project

### 4.3 Common Project Risks

- Schedule overruns
- Budget overruns
- Scope creep
- Resource unavailability
- Technical challenges
- Stakeholder misalignment
- Organizational change

## Chapter 5: Team Leadership (Pages 63-78)

### 5.1 Building Effective Teams

- Clear roles and responsibilities
- Psychological safety
- Shared goals and vision
- Regular communication
- Trust and respect

### 5.2 Leadership Styles

Different situations require different approaches:
- Directive: For unclear situations, new teams
- Participative: For experienced teams, uncertain direction
- Achievement-oriented: For routine work
- Supportive: For stressful work

### 5.3 Conflict Resolution

Conflicts are normal in projects. Approaches:
- Avoid: Not recommended for important issues
- Accommodate: Quick resolution but loses substance
- Compete: Winning is important, relationship suffers
- Compromise: Both sides give up something
- Collaborate: Win-win, best for important issues

## Chapter 6: Monitoring & Control (Pages 79-85)

### 6.1 Tracking Progress

- Compare actual to planned
- Earned Value Management
- Variance analysis
- Trend analysis

### 6.2 Control Actions

When deviations occur:
- Corrective actions: Get back on track
- Preventive actions: Avoid future problems
- Defect repair: Fix quality issues
